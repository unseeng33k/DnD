# THE TAMOACHAN EXPEDITION
## A Chronicle of the Descent into Jade and Darkness

## PROLOGUE: The Letter

The letter arrived on a grey morning, sealed with wax the color of old blood.

Malice Indarae De'Barazzan recognized the seal before she broke it—a symbol that belonged to no living scholar, yet here it was, pressed into the wax with unmistakable clarity. The hand that had made this mark had been dust for sixty years. Yet the script within was unmistakable: precise, economical, urgent.

*If you read this, I am dead. What I feared has come to pass. The Jade Temple calls. You must answer. You must go. Bring only those you trust, if you trust any at all.*

Malice read the letter three times before committing it to flame. The drow had learned, across nearly three centuries of existence, that trust was a luxury afforded only to those who could afford the cost of betrayal. She had been betrayed before. She would likely be betrayed again. But this—this was different.

The letter bore marks of power. Ancient power. The kind that reached across decades, from beyond death itself, with a single imperative: come.

And so she had begun to assemble a party.

A drow does not gather allies the way surface dwellers do. There is no talk of shared destiny, no rallying around banners and ideals. Instead, there are understandings. Transactions. The careful alignment of self-interest with momentary necessity.

Malice was a cleric of Lloth, the Spider Queen. She commanded spells of healing and smiting, of protection and revelation. But her truest gift lay in understanding what others wanted, and arranging for them to desire the same thing she did—at least for as long as it suited her purposes.

The temple called across the centuries. And Malice, for once, found herself genuinely curious about what would answer.

## BOOK ONE: THE SUMMONS

### CHAPTER I: The Jungle Approach

The jungle was alive with hunger.

Green canopy pressed down like the palm of some ancient and indifferent god, suffocating light, swallowing sound. Malice Indarae De'Barazzan stood at the jungle's edge, where the cultivated lands gave way to wilderness, and felt the weight of three hundred years of existence settle across her shoulders like a cloak sewn from stone.

The air itself seemed to writhe. Humid. Alive with the breath of things that crawled and climbed and hunted. Her darkvision painted the jungle in shades of grey and cold blue—a landscape of stone and shadow that the human eye could never fully comprehend. She understood its truth in a way surface dwellers never could. This place was *old*. Older than the stone of human kingdoms. Older than the memory of gods who had names.

Her hands found the holy symbol at her throat—a small, carved spider of black stone. Lloth's eternal truth, distant though the Spider Queen seemed in this humid, choking place, still hers to command. She could feel it: the faint thrumming of divine will, patient and eternal as a spider waiting in its web for vibrations that would tell it all it needed to know.

"The path lies ahead," a voice said behind her. Grond. The fighter was tense, as he always was when separation from civilized lands approached. His hand rested on the pommel of his sword—that reflexive gesture that meant uncertainty, or fear, or both.

Malice did not turn. "Then let us walk it."

The party had assembled over the course of a tenday. Grond first—a warrior of the old school, believing in codes and honor in an age when such things had become quaint. He had found her in a tavern in the civilized lands, seeking employment. She had given him a task, and a purpose. That had been enough.

The others had come in the way of such things. Recruited, as one recruits tools. Useful tools. Some of them might prove dangerous. Most tools did, if one wasn't careful.

The letter had been explicit: *bring only those you trust*. Malice had laughed at that, alone in her study, the parchment burning in her brazier. Trust. What a quaint concept. What she had brought instead were people whose interests aligned with hers, at least temporarily. In a dungeon—for this was surely a dungeon, whatever the letter claimed—temporary alignment was often enough.

The jungle trail opened before them like a wound in the green. Trees pressed close on either side, their ancient trunks covered in moss that seemed to pulse with life. The air stank of rot and growth in equal measure—the smell of things dying so that other things might live. The smell of age.

Malice moved forward, her hand on the wand at her hip. Behind her, she could hear the party: the clink of armor, the scrape of weapons, the heavy breathing of those who were not accustomed to such humidity. To such weight. To such hunger in the air.

The temple was close now. She could feel it the way a spider feels tremors in its web. Something ancient and aware lay ahead, waiting in the darkness beneath the jungle. And it had been waiting for someone like her.
